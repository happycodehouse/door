1979 - 1991
  • 1979

    George Lucas recruited Ed Catmull from NYIT to lead Lucasfilm's Computer Division, aiming to develop advanced computer technology for the film industry.

    Alvy Ray Smith joined as Director of Computer Graphics Research.

    Their goals included creating digital editing systems, a laser film printer, and exploring computer graphics.

  • 1982 -
    1983

    Lucasfilm's Computer Division completed the "Genesis Effect" sequence for "Star Trek II: The Wrath of Khan," the first fully computer-animated sequence in a feature film.

    The Graphics Group also created their first film-resolution image, "The Road to Point Reyes," and animator John Lasseter was invited to freelance with them.

  • 1984 -
    1985

    Lasseter is hired full-time, joining the Computer Division as an “Interface Designer.”
    They premiered a partially completed version of "The Adventures of André & Wally B." at SIGGRAPH, showcasing flexible characters, textures, and motion blur, marking a milestone in computer animation.
    The Computer Division also presented its prototype Pixar Image Computer at SIGGRAPH and completed the Stained Glass Knight sequence for the film "Young Sherlock Holmes."

  • 1986

    Steve Jobs bought the Computer Division from George Lucas and turned it into an independent company called "Pixar."
    Pixar and Disney started working together on CAPS (Computer Animation Production System), which would change how traditional animated movies were made.
    John Lasseter's short film "Luxo Jr." was finished and shown at SIGGRAPH in August, then in theaters in Los Angeles in November.

  • 1987 -
    1988

    "Red's Dream" premiered at SIGGRAPH, with parts rendered on the Pixar Image Computer.
    Pixar's animation system (Marionette/Menv) and renderer (RenderMan®) were internally released.
    "Tin Toy," created with these systems, won the Academy Award® for Best Short Film (Animated), a first for computer animation.

  • 1989

    "Knick Knack," their first stereoscopic 3D animation.
    The first commercial version of RenderMan® is released.
    Pixar starts making commercials, including a Tropicana ad directed by John Lasseter.
